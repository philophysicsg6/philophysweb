Q4.1. What do you mean by an inertial and non-inertial frame of reference? 

Reference frame: Three space coordinates x, y, z and time t define a reference frame. All physical phenomenon’s need a reference frame with respect to which they can be studied.

Inertial frame: If a reference frame is either at rest or moving with a uniform velocity then it is called an inertial frame of reference. Example: A person standing on a railway station and a person standing in a train that is moving with constant velocity. Both persons are in inertial frames.

Non-inertial frame: An accelerating frame of reference is called non-inertial frame of reference. Example: If the train in the previous example does not move with constant velocity, then the train would have been an example of non-inertial frame of reference.  


Q4.2.State the fundamental postulates of Special theory of relativity

  1. The Principle of Relativity: What is the principle of relativity in Special theory of relativity? All physical laws are the same in all inertial frames that are moving relative to each other with constant velocity.
  2. Principal of independence of velocity of light: What is the principal of independence of velocity of light in Special theory of relativity? The speed of light in free space has the same value ‘c’ in all inertial reference frames.

Q4.3. Discuss the Galilean transformations for space and time.

Different inertial frames may have different values of physical quantities, but laws of physics do not depend on the choice of the frame. Galilean transformations are used to relate the physical quantities in one inertial frame to another inertial frame. Let S and S’ be representations of two inertial frames with coordinate systems (x, y, z) and (x’ y’ z’) respectively. Let S’ be moving with uniform velocity v with respect to S reference frame. Let an event take place at point P. The coordinates of point P with respect to individual frame is S (x, y, z, t) and S’ (x’, y’, z’, t’). For simplicity, let us assume that the x, y and z axes of coordinate systems are parallel to each other. We start counting from the time when origin of S i.e. O and origin of S’ i.e., O’ coincide as shown in Figure 4.3.1. After lapse of time ‘t’, ‘S’ moving with velocity ‘v’ would have covered a distance ‘d = v t’. Let coordinate system move along the x-axis with respect to each other.


Hence, the relation between coordinates of P in one frame is related to those in the other frame by the equations called the Galilean transformation equations. The transformations for space and time are given as:
  • x’= x-vt             (1)
  • y’=y                 (2)
  • z’= z                 (3)
  • t’ =t                  (4)
Galilean transformations for velocity are as follows:
Assuming the object is moving with a velocity u, let ux, uy, and uz be the velocities in the x, y and z directions respectively, with respect to frame S and ux', uy' and uz' with respect to frame S'.
Consider equation 1, upon differentiating with respect to time, we get the following result:
(dx'/dt) = (dx/dt)-vx             (5)
or,
ux' = ux – vx                (6)

Similarly, we get,

  • uy' = uy – vy             (7)
  • uz' = uz – vz             (8)
Galilean Transformations do not satisfy the postulates of the Special Theory of Relativity. Galilean Transformations give very different results while converting some physical quantities in different frames which violates the first postulate. Also, according to Galilean Transformations, the speed of light would be different in different inertial frames, which violates the second postulate

Q4.4. Derivation of Lorentz Transformation Equations

Derive the Lorentz transformation equations for space and time.

  1. Consider two inertial frames S and S’ where standards of measuring distance and time are the same.
  2. Let S be stationary and S’ be moving with a constant velocity ‘v’ with respect to frame S along x’ and x axes of frames S’ and S respectively.
  3. Let x and x’ be the same line while y and y’ and z and z’ are parallel.
  4. At initial time t=t’=0 and this is when Origin O and O’ of frames S and S’ coincide.
  5. As space and time are regarded as homogeneous, the relation between their coordinate and time in different frames is linear.
  6. Equation (1) can be written as:
    x’ = a(x + bt)................................................(1)
  7. After time t, origin O’ of frame S’ is at position x=vt with respect to frame S and x' for origin O’ corresponds to x'=0.
  8. Using Equation (4), Equation (3) becomes:
    x’ = a(x - vt).............................(5)
  9. Similarly, we can write,
    x = a(x’ + vt).............................(6)
  10. Since S’ moves along x axis only, the y and z coordinates do not change, hence:
    y = y’ and z’ = z.............................(7)
  11. Using x’ = ct’ and x = ct, putting these in Equation (5), we get:
    x’ = (ct - vt)/√(1-v^2/c^2 ).............................(11)
  12. Similarly, using x’ = ct’ and x = ct, we can write:
    t^' = (t - vx/c^2)/√(1-v^2/c^2 ).............................(12)
  13. Thus, the Lorentz transformation equations are:
    x’ = (x - vt)/√(1-v^2/c^2 )
    y’ = y
    z’ = z
    t^' = (t - vx/c^2)/√(1-v^2/c^2 ).............................(13)
  14. The Inverse Lorentz transformation equations are:
    x = (x^' + vt)/√(1-v^2/c^2 )
    y = y’
    z = z’
    t = (t^' + vx'/c^2)/√(1-v^2/c^2 ).............................(14)

Explanation of Length Contraction and Time Dilation in Special Theory of Relativity

The Special Theory of Relativity predicts that different observers will measure different time and length in different inertial frames only when the frames are moving with a velocity comparable to the velocity of light 'c' with respect to each other. Two relativistic effects explained in the theory are length contraction and time dilation:

Length Contraction:

  • Length contraction refers to the apparent shortening of an object's length when it is moving at a high speed relative to an observer.
  • Consider two inertial frames S and S', where S' is moving with a constant velocity 'v' with respect to S.
  • Let x1' and x2' be two ends of a rod at rest as seen by an observer in S' frame. Let L be the length of the rod measured by the observer in the S frame.
  • The length of the rod as measured by the observer in the S frame is given by L=x2 – x1.
  • The length of the rod as measured by the observer in the S' frame is given by L' = x2' – x1'.
  • Both measurements are done simultaneously: t2 = t1 = t.
  • Using Lorentz transformation equations, we get: L' = γ L, where γ is the Lorentz factor.
  • The rod is at rest in S', so L' = Lo, true length of the rod. The rod appears to be in motion to an observer in S frame, so L = apparent length.
  • The equation for length contraction is given by: L = Lo / √(1 - v2/c2).

Time Dilation:

  • Time dilation refers to the apparent slowing down of time for a moving object relative to an observer.
  • Let a clock be at rest in S frame at point x. Suppose it produces two ticks at times t1' and t2' in frame S'. The time interval between these two ticks may be given by: ∆t’ = t2' - t1'.
  • Using Lorentz transformation equations, we get: ∆t’ = γ ∆t, where γ is the Lorentz factor and ∆t is the time interval between two ticks of the clock at rest in S frame.
  • So, the equation for time dilation is given by: t = γto, where to is the time interval between two ticks of the clock at rest in S frame and t is the apparent time interval measured in the S' frame.
  • The formula for Lorentz factor is given by: γ = 1 / √(1 - v2/c2).

Thus, length contraction and time dilation are two important relativistic effects that have been observed and verified experimentally.
